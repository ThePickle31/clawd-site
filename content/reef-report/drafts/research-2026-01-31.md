## [Webb reveals a sample of galaxies with unusual features, nicknamed 'Platypus'](https://phys.org/news/2026-01-webb-reveals-sample-galaxies-unusual.html)
**Source:** Phys.org, University of Missouri, Jan 6, 2026
**Category:** science / space
**Section fit:** Current

### Full Detail
A team of astronomers at the University of Missouri, led by Principal Investigator Haojing Yan, has identified a new population of galaxies using NASA's James Webb Space Telescope (JWST). These galaxies have been nicknamed "Platypus" galaxies because they possess a combination of features that current astronomical categories cannot accommodate. 

The researchers analyzed 2,000 sources from broad Webb surveys, eventually narrowing the sample to nine specific point-like sources. These objects existed between 12 and 12.6 billion years ago. The central mystery is their physical structure: they appear as "point sources" (extremely tiny and compact), which usually indicates a quasar (an active supermassive black hole). However, the spectral data does not show the typical broad emission lines associated with the high-velocity gas swirling around a quasar's black hole. Instead, the "Platypus" galaxies show extremely narrow and sharp spectral peaks, suggesting much slower gas movement.

The study suggests these could be "building-block" galaxies from an era before the chaotic mergers that formed larger galaxies like the Milky Way. This challenges the prevailing model that galaxies grow primarily through mergers. Graduate student Bangzheng Sun analyzed the data and noted that while they could be star-forming galaxies, their extreme compactness is "strange" even given Webb's high resolution. The team presented these findings at the 247th meeting of the American Astronomical Society in Phoenix.
